Abstract

Structural DNA technology such as DNA origami and DNA tile is successfully employed in the construction of dimensional structures, which serve as crucial nanoreactors both for protein encapsulation and natural protein assembly. Previously, Udomprasert et al. had designed a tubular DNA origami and endowed it with nucleation site to induce the formation of amyloid fibrils. Inspired by this success, herein, we have designed and constructed a compact tetrahedral-like DNA cage, which can be converted to an unconfined cage after reaction with a fuel strand. When an internal DNA staple is hybridized with a DNA strand that contains a specific genome RNA sequence of tobacco mosaic virus (TMV), called origin of assembly, the RNA could induce the formation of TMV protein nanodisk within the DNA cage. Our research could be employed to construct nanoreactors for more sophisticated protein assemblies and the resulting assemblies will shed light on the principle of protein assembly-associated diseases, such as Alzheimer's and Parkinson's diseases.

Introduction

DNA origami has been widely applied in various fields in the past decade due to its programmability in structural design.1-3 DNA origami can be constructed based on a scaffold (a single DNA strand derived from plasmid) and numerous ssDNA staples, leading to nanoscale DNA boxes, walkers, tweezers.4-6 Several studies have indicated that DNA nanodevice could be used for binding, encapsulation and release of certain protein, demonstrating the feasibility and potential of DNA nanostructures in protein-related applications.7-10 For instance, the capture of enzyme horseradish peroxidase by DNA cage has been achieved via the denaturation and refolding of DNA hairpin by temperature alteration, which provides a new path for DNA-cage-mediated protein encapsulation.10 However, the DNA cage with the limited size could only capture single small proteins instead of multiple proteins especially naturally protein assemblies, which find applications in vaccine and biomedicine.11-13 Thus, design of DNA nanocage with enhanced cavity for seizure of protein assemblies would be of great interest.

Great efforts have been devoted to constructing DNA-protein assemblies for multiple purposes, with a wide range of strategies emerging, such as biotin-streptavidin,14 aptamer-protein,15, antigen-antibody,16 junction-binding proteins,17 oligonucleotide linkers,18 polyamide recognition,19 amyloid self-assembly,11 and etc. Recently, new strategies of virus DNA-guided protein assembly came into view and raised many researchers’ interest.20 Although one-dimensional DNA-templated protein assembly strategy has been adopted to construct virus-like nanotube, there’s no report about virus coat protein (CP) binding to DNA nanodevice,13 which let us sketch out an idea of CP encapsulation by DNA cage.

Tobacco mosaic virus (TMV) has attracted enormous attention in self-assembly technology by utilizing the specific recognition between TMV RNA and CP subunits.21-22 The strong and precise interaction of CPs to a characteristic RNA loop (origin of assembly, OA) is believed as the start-up of TMV self-assembly, which is often employed to build programmed biomolecular materials.23-25 Hybridizing the OA-containing RNA with modified DNA had facilitated the controllable synthesis and regulation of protein-inorganic composites: the TMV-liked nanorod,26 the nanostar colloids of gold core with TMV-liked tubes27 and the TMV derived nanotube array28 were reported before. Therefore, it’s entirely possible to modify DNA origami with functional RNA for additional versatility.

Inspired by the convenient RNA-directed bottom-up assembly strategy, we introduce the OA RNA into a three-dimensional DNA origami structure for virus protein detection without any chemical modification on DNA strand. In this study, we chose tetrahedron, the simplest structure among all of polyhedrons, for DNA cage design. The elaborate cage can be opened and closed manually by adding and removing ‘fuel’ strand of DNA that controls CPs entry. One protruding strand of OA-containing RNA was added into the cage to detect CPs and induce the subsequent self-assembly. We expect that our boldly proposed design can contribute to the enrichment of DNA-origami-based protein encapsulation and give some inspirations for new design of reconfigurable three-dimensional DNA nanostructure.
